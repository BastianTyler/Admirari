First Law of Thermodynamics

The first law of thermodynamics states in so few words that the total energy of a closed system remains constant.

The first law in detail!

There is a lot surrounding the first law that we should discuss prior to getting to the equation. When we are doing problems involving thermodynamics we are attempting to model some thing in the real world to our mathamatical models. In the case of gases, trying to completly understand the movement of \(10^{23}\) gas particles is unreasonable. Instead we try to look at averages. In more scientific speech: we focus on a macrosscopic description of the microscopic particles.

Lets define some terms before moving on.

  • System: A quantity of matter around which we can draw a boundary the matter will not cross. This implies a closed system, where the number of molecules in the system is constant.
  • Thermodynamic State & State Variables: Measurable variables and properties that can be used to determine other variables and properties. P,V, and T (Pressure, Volume, and Temperature) are examples. An extensive property depends on tt he total amount of a substance while and intensive propertie does not depend on the total amount of the substance.
  • Equations of State: Connects state variables together.
  • Thermodynamic Equilibrium: The state of the system is unchanging.
  • Thermodynamic Processes: Changes the state of a system. We will analyze processes changing at an infintesimal rate. This means whils the gases are changing they are essentially stuck in equallibrium. We can call this quasi-equallibrium.
Lets look at the equations.

First we should discuss the work involved with the gas. When the volume of the system the gas is inside expands then the gas will have dont positive work, but if the gas is compressed it will have done negative work. This is stated as work done by gas on the piston. $$W_{\text{by gas on piston}} = \int_{i}^{f}P dV$$

  • P Pressure of the gas
  • dV differential volume changes
  • \(W_{\text{by gas on piston}}\) \(>0\) as expands, \(>0\) as contracts. \(W_{\text{by gas on piston}} = -W_{\text{on gas by piston}}\)

The First Law of Thermodynamic is

$$\Delta E_{\text{internal of gas}} = Q_{\text{added to gas}} + W_{\text{on gas}}$$

Or simply

$$\Delta E_{\text{int}} = Q_{\text{in}} + W_{\text{on}}$$

From this we can gather taht as you do work or add heat to a gas its internal energy will increase. Often we care about the power output of an engine. For these problems use:

$$\Delta E_{\text{int}} = Q_{\text{in}} - W_{\text{by}}$$
