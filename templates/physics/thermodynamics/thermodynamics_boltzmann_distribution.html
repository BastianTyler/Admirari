Boltzmann Distribution

So, a while ago boltzmann discovered the meanss of finding the number of gas molecules per unit volume between energy states:

$$n_v(E) = n_0 e^{-\frac{E}{k_bT}}$$

where \(n_v(E)\) is the number of molecules per unit volume with energy beween 0 and dE. If we were to integrate over all possible energy values we could get total number of molecules in the gas! Remember, N represents the total number of molecules in a gas. $$ N = \int_{E_i}^{E_f} n_0e^{-\frac{E}{k_bT}} \,dE$$

In order to get a Probability Function out of this we could divide both values by N.

Maxwell-Boltzmann Speed Distribution Function

Supposing that the energy of molecules can be associated purely with kinetic energy, such as in an ideal gas, we then can get a probability distribution for molecular speed. $$ f(v)dv = (\frac{m}{2\pi k_bT}){^\frac{3}{2}}e^{-\frac{mv^2}{2k_bT}}4\pi v^2dv $$ where:

  • m mass of molecule
  • T temperature in kelvin
  • v molecular speed
  • \(4\pi v^2\) comes from converting from 3D Cartesian to 3D Sphereical coordinates.
  • \((\frac{m}{2\pi k_bT}){^\frac{3}{2}}\) is a normalization constant required to ensure \(\int_{i}^{f} f(v)dv = 1\)

The area under the distribution function in any inerval represents the probability of the gas having speeds in that range.

Some helpfull integrals

These integrals should help for certain problems when calculating average speed. They are only valid for limits of \(x = 0\) to \(x = \infty \)

$$ I_0 = \int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-ax^2}dx = \frac{1}{2} \sqrt{\frac{\pi }{a}} $$ $$ I_1 = \int_{0}^{\infty} xe^{-ax^2}dx = \frac{1}{2a} $$ $$ I_2 = \int_{0}^{\infty} x^2e^{-ax^2}dx = \frac{1}{4} \sqrt{\frac{\pi }{a^3}} $$ $$ I_3 = \int_{0}^{\infty} x^3e^{-ax^2}dx = \frac{1}{2a^2} $$ $$ I_4 = \int_{0}^{\infty} x^4e^{-ax^2}dx = \frac{3}{8} \sqrt{\frac{\pi }{a^5}} $$ $$ I_5 = \int_{0}^{\infty} x^5e^{-ax^2}dx = \frac{1}{a^3} $$ $$ I_{2n} = \int_{0}^{\infty} x^{2n}e^{-ax^2}dx = \frac{1}{2} \sqrt{\frac{\pi }{a}} $$ $$ I_{2n+1} = \int_{0}^{\infty} x^{2n+1}e^{-ax^2}dx = \frac{1}{2} \sqrt{\frac{\pi }{a}} $$
