Heat

Is it getting hot in here, or is it specifically me?

Heat can be a frustruating

Remember a few things:

  • A lot of these terms relate to heat, but they are not in the same units of heat
  • Heat can be positive or negative. Positive corresponds to a temperature increase. Negative corresponds to a temperature decrease. This means in the following equations negative heat implies \(\Delta T < 0\), and similar for positive.
  • Check units

Specific Heat

Formula: $$ c = (\frac{1}{m})(\frac{dQ}{dT}) $$

This is the heat required to raise the tempature of 1 kg of material by 1K = 1\(^\circ C\)

Units: \( \frac{J}{kg K}\) or \(\frac{J}{kg^\circ C}\)

Heat Capacity

Formula: $$ C = mc $$

This is the heat required to raise the tempature of an object by 1K = 1\(^\circ C\)

Units: \(\frac{J}{K}\) or \(\frac{J}{^\circ C}\)

Specific Latent Heat

This is the energy per unit mass required to cause a phase change. The idea is that there wont be a measureable \(\Delta T\) durring the transition of, say solid ice to liquid water, so the energy is hidden or 'latent'.

Units: \(\frac{J}{kg}\) or \(\frac{J}{mol}\)

Use subscripts of L to denote type of phase change.

  • \(L_f\) for latent heat of fusion. Use for melting/freezing problems
  • \(L_v\) for latent heat of vaporization. Use for boiling/condensing problems

From what we just covered we can define two equations for heat

During temperature changes $$ Q = mc \Delta T = C\Delta T $$

During phase changes $$ Q = \begin{matrix} + \\ - \end{matrix}mL $$ Sign is determined by type of change. Melting ice means \(Q = +m_{ice}L_f\)

Specific Heats of Gases

Specific heat for gasses differs if done at constant pressure \(v_p\) or at constant volume \(c_v\)

Thigns to note

  • Regions with zero slope indicate phase changes
  • Regions with non-zero slopes indicate temerature changes
  • Smaller values of c casue steeper slopes, thus small c indicates an easy to change T
  • Objects that are cooled should expect Q < 0
