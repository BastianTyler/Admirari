Row Echelon Form

This page covers various operations that can be performed on matrices in linear algebra.

Description of the matrix operation goes here.

Fun note, name comes from french word for stair case.

A row-echelon matrix must but a staircase of ones over zeros.

$$ \begin{aligned} x_1 + 2x_2 + 3x_3 &= 5 \\ \quad \quad x_2 + 4x_3 &= 6 \\ \quad \quad \quad \quad x_3 &= 2 \end{aligned} $$ $$ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 & | 5 \\ 0 & 1 & 4 & | 6 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & | 2 \end{bmatrix} $$

Are these row-echelon matricies?

$$ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -3 & 0 & 7 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 4 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$ $$ \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} $$

Yes, yes they are. But what about these row-echelon matrices?

$$ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 2 & 1 & 6 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$ $$ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & 3 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 & 4 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} $$

Nope! You fall into zeros on your staircase, or it isnt over all zeros!

Elementary Row Operations

Something handy, all linear system of equations can be augmented and reduced into row-echelon form!

The are three operations we will use.
  1. Permutation
  2. Scalar Row Multiplication
  3. Addition of Two Rows
Permutation

Permutation is just reordering the rows. Simple right.

Often, as we will soon see, this is helpful to get a 1 in place

$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 & 9 \end{bmatrix} $$ $$ A_{1,2} $$ $$ A = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{1} & \mathbf{2} & \mathbf{3} \\ \mathbf{4} & \mathbf{5} & \mathbf{6} \\ 7 & 8 & 9 \end{bmatrix} $$ $$ A' = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 7 & 8 & 9 \end{bmatrix} $$
Scalar Row Multipication

Sclar Row Multipication just means we mutiple all elements in the row by some scalar value. This is also how we 'divide'.

Use it to clear fractions, or whatever else you want

$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 & 9 \end{bmatrix} $$ $$ A_{2}^{(3)} $$ $$ A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ \mathbf{4} & \mathbf{5} & \mathbf{6} \\ 7 & 8 & 9 \end{bmatrix} $$ $$ 3 A_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \cdot 3 & 5 \cdot 3 & 6 \cdot 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 12 & 15 & 18 \end{bmatrix} $$ $$ A' = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 12 & 15 & 18 \\ 7 & 8 & 9 \end{bmatrix} $$
Addition of Two Rows

The most difficult to master and get a feel for. Addition of two rows means we take two row vectors, a and b. We will add the elements of a to b, changing b in the process and leaving a alone in the end result. The elements are added directely across their closes row.

We can also multiply a by some scalar value before adding it to b. Like before, b is changed, but a remains unchanged in the final result.

Yeah thats a lot, so we will look at it closely.

Often, as we will soon see, this is helpful to get a 1 in place

$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 & 9 \end{bmatrix} $$ $$ A_{2,1}^{(2)} $$ $$ A = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{1} & \mathbf{2} & \mathbf{3} \\ \mathbf{4} & \mathbf{5} & \mathbf{6} \\ 7 & 8 & 9 \end{bmatrix} $$ $$ A_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix}, \quad A_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 5 & 6 \end{bmatrix} $$ $$ 2 A_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 & 6 \end{bmatrix} $$ $$ A_2 + 2A_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 4+2 & 5+4 & 6+6 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 6 & 9 & 12 \end{bmatrix} $$ $$ A' = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 6 & 9 & 12 \\ 7 & 8 & 9 \end{bmatrix} $$
Simple example of getting into row-echelon form

When possible aim to get a left most 1 in the upper corner. Do this left to right, getting to row of the execept its leading 1. Then move on to the next, rasing a 1 to he right spot and using it to create what you need.
